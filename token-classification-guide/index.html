Token Classification: How European Regulation Categorizes Digital Assets

How to classify your token under European regulation. Security token, utility token, ART, EMT — classification criteria, regulatory consequences, and common mistakes.

Why Classification Matters

Token classification determines which regulatory framework applies. A security token is governed by MiFID II, the Prospectus Regulation, and CSDR — the full weight of securities regulation. A utility token under MiCA faces a lighter whitepaper regime. An ART or EMT faces specific authorization and reserve requirements. A misclassified token faces enforcement action.

Classification is not a choice. It is a legal determination based on the characteristics of the token, the rights it confers, and how it is offered to the public. Calling a token "utility" does not make it one if it carries economic rights typical of a security.

Security Token Indicators

A token is likely a security if it: confers ownership rights in a company or fund, entitles holders to a share of profits or revenues, creates an expectation of financial return derived from the efforts of others, represents a debt obligation with repayment terms, or is freely transferable and traded on secondary markets.

The EU does not apply the US Howey test directly, but the analytical approach is similar: substance over form. National competent authorities across the EU have published guidance, and ESMA has issued opinions on crypto-asset classification that reinforce the primacy of economic substance.

MiCA Classifications

Asset-Referenced Token (ART): maintains stable value by referencing the value of several fiat currencies, one or more commodities, one or more crypto-assets, or a combination. The key test is multiple reference assets.

E-Money Token (EMT): purports to maintain stable value by referencing the value of one single official currency. Single fiat reference = EMT.

Utility Token: intended to provide digital access to a good or service, available on DLT, and accepted only by the issuer of that token. Must have a genuine functional purpose beyond speculation.

Catch-all: crypto-assets that do not qualify as ART, EMT, utility, or financial instruments still fall under MiCA Title II whitepaper requirements.

Hybrid Tokens

Many tokens do not fit neatly into one category. A token that provides access to a platform (utility) but also entitles holders to a share of platform revenue (security) is hybrid. Regulators assess the dominant characteristic.

The safe approach: if any security characteristic is present, treat it as a security unless you have a clear legal basis for another classification. Downward classification (treating a potential security as a utility) is the most common and most dangerous mistake.

Getting Classification Right

Classification requires analysis of: the rights attached to the token (legal and economic), the expectations created by marketing materials, the structure of any offering, secondary market trading arrangements, and the nature of any underlying asset or service.
