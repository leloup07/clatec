CASP Authorization: What Services Require a License Under MiCA

Complete guide to Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) authorization under MiCA. Service categories, organizational requirements, passporting, and the authorization process.

What Is a CASP?

A Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) is any legal person or undertaking whose occupation or business is the provision of one or more crypto-asset services to clients on a professional basis. The definition is in MiCA Article 3(1)(15).

The critical word is "professional basis." Providing crypto-asset services occasionally, or only to yourself, does not trigger CASP obligations. But any regular, commercial provision of covered services to third parties requires authorization.

The Ten Service Categories

MiCA Article 3(1)(16) defines ten categories of crypto-asset services. Each triggers specific obligations:

Custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients (Art. 75) — safekeeping of clients' crypto-assets or means of access. Strict segregation and liability rules apply.

Operation of a trading platform for crypto-assets (Art. 76) — managing a multilateral system where multiple third-party buying and selling interests interact.

Exchange of crypto-assets for funds (Art. 77) — facilitating the exchange of crypto-assets for fiat currency. Requires non-discriminatory commercial policy.

Exchange of crypto-assets for other crypto-assets (Art. 77) — same framework, applied to crypto-to-crypto exchange.

Execution of orders on behalf of clients (Art. 78) — concluding agreements to purchase or sell crypto-assets on behalf of clients.

Placing of crypto-assets (Art. 79) — marketing of newly-issued crypto-assets to purchasers.

Reception and transmission of orders (Art. 80) — receiving an order and transmitting it for execution.

Providing advice on crypto-assets (Art. 81) — offering personalized recommendations on transactions.

Providing portfolio management of crypto-assets (Art. 82) — managing portfolios on a discretionary, client-by-client basis.

Providing transfer services for crypto-assets on behalf of clients (Art. 83) — transferring crypto-assets between DLT addresses.

Authorization Process

Applications are submitted to the competent authority of the home member state. Required documentation includes: program of operations, governance arrangements, proof of good repute and competence of management, business continuity plan, internal control mechanisms, AML/CFT procedures, and proof of minimum prudential requirements.

The competent authority has up to 40 working days to assess completeness, then 25 working days to grant or refuse authorization. ESMA maintains a public register of all authorized CASPs.

Passporting

Once authorized in one EU member state, a CASP may provide services across all EU/EEA states through passporting (Articles 65-66). The home competent authority notifies the host state authority, and cross-border activity can commence. No separate authorization is required in the host state.

Passporting does not apply to third-country firms. A firm established outside the EU cannot passport into the EU through a MiCA authorization.

Do You Need CASP Authorization?

If you provide any of the ten listed services to clients on a professional basis from within the EU, authorization is required. The analysis is service-by-service: you may need authorization for some activities but not others.
