MiCA Regulation: What It Covers, Who It Affects, and How to Comply

Comprehensive guide to the EU Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA). Scope, token classifications, whitepaper requirements, CASP authorization, exemptions, and transitional provisions.

What Is MiCA?

The Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2023/1114) is the European Union's comprehensive framework for crypto-assets that are not already regulated as financial instruments under MiFID II. It entered into force in June 2023, with full application from December 2024.

MiCA creates a unified regulatory framework across all EU member states, replacing the patchwork of national approaches that previously governed crypto-assets. It covers three main areas: rules for token issuance (including whitepapers), specific regimes for asset-referenced tokens (ART) and e-money tokens (EMT), and authorization requirements for crypto-asset service providers (CASP).

Scope: What Falls Under MiCA?

MiCA applies to crypto-assets that are not already classified as financial instruments, deposits, structured deposits, or securitisation positions. The key exclusion is Article 2(4): tokens qualifying as transferable securities under MiFID II fall outside MiCA entirely and are governed by existing securities regulation.

Other exclusions include: central bank digital currencies, crypto-assets issued by central banks acting as monetary authority, deposits, and certain insurance products. NFTs are generally excluded unless they are fungible in practice or exhibit financial instrument characteristics — a grey area that requires case-by-case analysis.

Token Categories Under MiCA

Asset-Referenced Tokens (Title III, Articles 16-47): tokens that maintain stable value by referencing multiple assets, currencies, or commodities. Issuers must be authorized, maintain reserves, and offer redemption rights. "Significant" ARTs face enhanced requirements.

E-Money Tokens (Title IV, Articles 48-58): tokens referencing a single official currency. Must be issued by authorized credit institutions or electronic money institutions. Redemption at par value must be available at any time.

Other crypto-assets (Title II, Articles 4-15): all crypto-assets that are neither ART, EMT, nor financial instruments. Subject to whitepaper requirements including content specifications, notification to competent authorities, and marketing communications rules.

CASP Authorization

Title V (Articles 59-83) requires authorization for entities providing crypto-asset services within the EU. Ten service categories are defined: custody and administration, trading platform operation, exchange services, order execution, placing, reception and transmission of orders, advice, portfolio management, and transfer services.

Authorization is granted by the home member state competent authority and allows passporting across the EU. Organizational, prudential, and conduct of business requirements apply. Transitional provisions allowed existing providers to continue operating temporarily, but new authorizations are now required.

Exemptions and Transitional Provisions

Article 143 provided transitional arrangements for CASPs already operating before MiCA's application date. The duration varied by member state implementation.

Small-scale offerings may benefit from simplified requirements. Intra-group transactions and tokens distributed free of charge have specific exemption conditions. Each exemption has precise conditions that must be verified against the specific facts.

Does MiCA Apply to Your Project?

The answer depends on: what your token represents, how it maintains value (if at all), whether you provide crypto-asset services, where your users are located, and whether any exclusion applies to your specific structure.
