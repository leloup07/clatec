How to Structure Asset Tokenization Legally in Europe

Understand the legal and regulatory requirements for asset tokenization in Europe. Token classification, issuer obligations, investor protection, and platform requirements under MiCA and national frameworks.

What Is Asset Tokenization?

Asset tokenization is the process of representing ownership rights in a real-world asset — real estate, debt instruments, fund interests, commodities, or other financial assets — as digital tokens on a distributed ledger. The token does not change the nature of the underlying asset; it changes how ownership is recorded, transferred, and potentially fractionalized.

The regulatory implications depend not on the technology used, but on what the token represents and how it is offered. A token representing a share in a company is still a security. A token representing a right to repayment is still a debt instrument. The DLT layer is infrastructure, not a regulatory exemption.

Token Classification: The First Question

Before any structuring decision, the token must be classified. European regulation distinguishes between several categories, each carrying different obligations:

Security tokens — tokens that qualify as transferable securities under MiFID II fall outside MiCA and are regulated under the Prospectus Regulation, MiFID II, and CSDR. This is the most demanding regulatory path.

Asset-referenced tokens (ART) — tokens maintaining stable value by referencing multiple assets. Regulated under MiCA Title III with authorization, reserve, and redemption requirements.

E-money tokens (EMT) — tokens referencing a single fiat currency. Regulated under MiCA Title IV with e-money legislation applying in parallel.

Utility tokens — tokens providing access to a service on DLT. Subject to lighter MiCA whitepaper obligations under Title II.

Classification is fact-specific. Hybrid tokens — those exhibiting characteristics of more than one category — require careful analysis because misclassification triggers either over-regulation or regulatory breach.

Key Regulatory Considerations

Issuer obligations vary by token type. Security token issuers face prospectus requirements unless an exemption applies. MiCA token issuers must publish a crypto-asset whitepaper meeting specific content requirements.

Investor protection frameworks determine who can participate. Retail investor exposure triggers the most stringent requirements under both MiFID II and MiCA, including suitability assessments, risk warnings, and potential cooling-off periods.

Platform requirements affect where and how tokens can be traded. Tokenized securities require regulated trading venues. MiCA crypto-assets can be traded on CASP-operated platforms.

Cross-border implications arise whenever issuance, distribution, or trading involves more than one jurisdiction. Passporting under MiCA or MiFID II may be available, but substance requirements and reverse solicitation rules constrain structuring options.

The DLT Pilot Regime

Regulation (EU) 2022/858 creates a temporary regime allowing market infrastructures to test DLT-based trading and settlement with targeted exemptions from MiFID II and CSDR. Three categories exist: DLT MTF, DLT SS, and DLT TSS. The regime operates for six years with value thresholds limiting the size of instruments that qualify.

Common Structuring Mistakes

Classifying a token as "utility" when it carries economic rights that make it a security.

Assuming that tokenization on a private blockchain avoids regulation — it does not if the token is offered to the public or admitted to trading.

Neglecting the distinction between issuing entity and platform operator — combining both roles creates conflicts of interest that regulators scrutinize.

Structuring across jurisdictions without substance — shell entities without real operations invite regulatory challenge.

When Professional Review Is Required

Any tokenization involving retail investors, economic rights, cross-border distribution, or significant issuance size typically requires formal legal review before implementation. The regulatory surface is too broad for preliminary analysis alone.
